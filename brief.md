## What Is Happening Right Now

On February 28, 2026, the US launched Operation Epic Fury against Iran. The Strait of Hormuz — a narrow water passage near Iran where roughly 20% of the world's oil and gas passes through every single day — has been effectively shut down. We are now on Day 32 of this crisis.

The numbers are staggering. Daily ship transits through the Strait dropped from 138 to just 2. That is a 98% disruption. There are 1,900 vessels stranded. 20,000 seafarers are trapped. Oil prices have surged from $75 to over $104 per barrel. Tanker day rates hit an all-time record of $423,736.

Qatar, one of the world's largest LNG and fertilizer exporters, declared force majeure at its 77 million-ton-per-year Ras Laffan facility. This means Qatar legally cannot deliver fertilizer to global markets.

## Why CF Industries Wins

CF Industries makes nitrogen-based fertilizer — ammonia, urea, and UAN — the stuff farmers need to grow crops. They are headquartered in Deerfield, Illinois, and they are one of the largest fertilizer producers in North America.

Here is the key insight: CF uses cheap American natural gas as their primary input. US Henry Hub natural gas costs $2.89 per MMBtu. Their overseas competitors have to use imported LNG, which now costs over $15 per MMBtu — more than 5 times higher. This gives CF the widest cost advantage in over a decade.

Think of it like an umbrella store during a rainstorm. CF does not sell oil. They are not directly exposed to the Hormuz crisis. But because the crisis has shut down Qatar's fertilizer exports and driven up energy costs for every competitor outside the US, CF's domestic production becomes incredibly valuable. They fill the gap that Qatar left behind.

## Where Wall Street Is Wrong

Wall Street consensus has CF earning $30.77 per share in FY2026. Their models assume the Hormuz crisis resolves by late April or early May and that oil prices normalize. But prediction markets — websites where people bet real money on what they think will happen — tell a very different story.

On Polymarket, there is a 68% probability that US forces enter Iran by April 30. On Kalshi, the probability that the Strait of Hormuz returns to normal traffic by April 15 is only 25%. There is a 52% chance that WTI oil hits $120 per barrel in April.

These are real people betting real money. And their forecasts are dramatically more pessimistic than what Wall Street analysts are assuming.

If the disruption persists through the Q2 planting season — when farmers worldwide are buying fertilizer — CF's earnings could reach $38 to $50 per share, well above the $30.77 consensus. Fertilizer prices could spike 30 to 50%. Revenue could hit $8 to $9 billion versus the $6.96 billion analysts expect.

## Scenario Analysis

We model four scenarios:

Bear case, 20% probability: The crisis ends quickly. Peace deal happens, Strait reopens, oil drops to $70-80. CF earns $22-25 EPS. Stock goes to $95-110. We lose about 20%.

Base case, 45% probability: Prolonged partial closure. Oil stays at $95-115. CF earns $35-40 EPS. Stock goes to $155-175. We gain about 25%.

Bull case, 25% probability: Extended full closure through summer. Oil hits $120-140. CF earns $42-50 EPS. Stock goes to $185-220. We gain about 55%.

Tail case, 10% probability: A second chokepoint gets disrupted (like the Suez or Bab el-Mandeb). Oil hits $140-170. CF earns $50-60+ EPS. Stock could reach $220-280. We gain about 90%.

The probability-weighted expected return is approximately +30%.
